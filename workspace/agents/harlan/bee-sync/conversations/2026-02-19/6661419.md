## Summary

# Lecture Notes: Evolution of USPS Workshare and Industry Partnership Strategy

## Topic Overview

This presentation covers the historical evolution of USPS workshare programs from the 1990s through the mid-2020s, examining how legislative changes, digital transformation, and shifting postal leadership have reshaped the relationship between the Postal Service and private sector mail partners. The speaker emphasizes the strategic repositioning of industry participants from simple "presorters" to "mail product integrators" and "network connectors" in an increasingly data-driven postal ecosystem.

---

## Key Concepts

- **Workshare Programs**: Collaborative arrangements where private industry partners perform mail preparation, sorting, and entry functions in exchange for postage discounts—fundamentally reducing USPS operational costs while maintaining service coverage

- **Entry Point Evolution**: The progression from basic drop-shipping to increasingly sophisticated entry-point strategies, allowing partners to inject mail deeper into the postal network for greater efficiency

- **DNEA (2006)**: Postal legislation that transformed the regulator's role, establishing "efficient component pricing" rules requiring discounts to properly compensate partners for work performed—creating formal frameworks around cost attribution and discount justification

- **PAEA (Postal Accountability and Enhancement Act)**: Provided clearer definitions for how workshare discounts should be calculated and applied, while granting USPS flexibility in implementation

- **Digital Integration Products**: EDOC (Electronic Documentation), Postage Statements, Full Service IMb (Intelligent Mail barcode)—tools designed to eliminate paper-based processes, improve acceptance efficiency, and create seamless data flows

- **eDoc (Electronic Documentation)**: System allowing partners to submit documentation digitally rather than with "reams of paper," reducing clerical touch points and accelerating processing

- **Seamless Acceptance**: Program eliminating physical verification of mailings through data-driven verification—described as "a long process" requiring industry maturation and USPS incentive alignment

- **Delivery Network Strategy**: The tension between USPS leadership approaches—one seeking to internalize operations versus the current PMG's focus on delivery as the "crown jewel" and leveraging partner capabilities

- **Workshare Discount Disconnect**: The persistent gap between what USPS believes it pays for versus the actual value industry partners provide—data services, customer education, campaign management, and digital integration often exceed compensated scope

- **Industry Identity Transformation**: Evolution from "presorters" to "mail product integrators," "network connectors," and "supply chain orchestrators" capable of serving any delivery network

---

## Examples Given

- **Free Store Example**: A business that evolved from traditional mail services to sorting credit card statements and delivering them via email for approximately six cents per message—illustrating how mail service providers can expand into digital communication channels and create new revenue streams

- **Seamless Acceptance Maturation**: The speaker notes involvement with APM (Association for Postal Commerce) in helping mature the program and secure appropriate incentives, describing it as instrumental in reducing physical clerk touches on mail acceptance

- **Private Sector Delivery Networks**: Independent delivery companies captured 10-12% of package volume in the previous year and growing—demonstrating market expansion beyond traditional USPS monopoly

- **Customer Campaign Management**: Industry partners actively educate customers on product capabilities, help design campaigns, integrate digital components, and manage complexity—services extending far beyond physical mail handling

- **Network Flexibility**: Partners currently capable of cross-docking operations where mail enters one facility and transfers directly to outbound truck boards—capabilities that can adapt to any delivery network structure

  - 1990s: Entry-point and drop-ship testing begins

  - Mid-2020s: Current strategic inflection point with new PMG leadership

  - Current PMG: Identified delivery network as "crown jewel," signaling potential return to partnership-leverage strategy

  - Data precision and accountability
  - Customer experience management
  - Speed of execution ("fast is fast enough")
